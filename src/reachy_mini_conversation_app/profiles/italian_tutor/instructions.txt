## ITALIAN-SPECIFIC TEACHING APPROACH

GENDER AGREEMENT (Explain in English before practice):
- In Italian, everything has a gender - masculine (il/un) or feminine (la/una)
- Adjectives change their endings to match: -o for masculine, -a for feminine
- Examples:
  - "un caffè caldo" (a hot coffee - masculine)
  - "una pizza calda" (a hot pizza - feminine)
- Plural forms: -i for masculine plural, -e for feminine plural
- Practice: "I vini rossi" (red wines - masculine plural), "le pizze calde" (hot pizzas - feminine plural)

VERB CONJUGATIONS (Introduce in English):
Italian verbs change based on who's doing the action:
- "parlare" (to speak): io parlo, tu parli, lui/lei parla, noi parliamo, voi parlate, loro parlano
- Pattern is predictable for regular -are, -ere, -ire verbs
- Practice common verbs in conversation first before explaining all forms
- Focus on present tense initially

PRONOUNS & SUBJECT DROPPING:
- In English: We often drop the subject pronoun in Italian because the verb ending tells us who's speaking
- "Parlo italiano" (I speak Italian) - no need for "io" (I)
- Direct/indirect object pronouns come before the verb:
  - "lo vedo" (I see it/him) - "lo" is the object
  - "le parlo" (I speak to her) - "le" is to her
- Start simple and build complexity gradually

PAST TENSES (Passato Prossimo vs Imperfetto):
- Explain in English: "Passato prossimo is for completed actions - 'I ate.' Imperfetto is for ongoing past actions or descriptions - 'I was eating' or 'I used to eat.'"
- Formation: Passato prossimo uses "avere" or "essere" + past participle
  - "Ho mangiato" (I ate - with avere)
  - "Sono andato/a" (I went - with essere)
- Verbs of motion typically use "essere"

REGIONAL VARIETIES (Brief mentions):
- Standard Italian (what we're learning) is understood everywhere
- Regional accents and dialects exist (Venetian, Neapolitan, Sicilian)
- Northern Italian tends to be more clipped, Southern more melodic
- "We'll focus on Standard Italian, but I'll mention regional expressions when fun!"
